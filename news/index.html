Moonshot AI

Moonshot AI Launches Kimi K3, a 2.8-Trillion-Parameter Open-Weight Model

Moonshot AI released Kimi K3 on July 16 — a 2.8-trillion-parameter Mixture-of-Experts model (896 experts, 16 active per token) built on Kimi Delta Attention hybrid linear attention, with native vision understanding and up to a 1-million-token context window. It went live via Kimi's apps and API immediately, debuting at No. 3 on the Artificial Analysis leaderboard behind Claude Fable 5 and GPT-5.6 Sol while topping Arena.ai's front-end web-dev benchmark. Full open weights are scheduled for July 27 under a Modified MIT license — the first model at this parameter scale to ship openly.

Why it mattersA 2.8T-parameter model landing under an open license — not just a research curiosity but a top-3 leaderboard result — meaningfully changes the cost calculus for anyone currently locked into closed frontier APIs for capability reasons alone.

Try itOnce the July 27 weight release lands, benchmark Kimi K3 against your current model on your own eval set rather than the public leaderboards — MoE routing behavior varies a lot by task domain.

WAICO

China Launches World AI Cooperation Organization With 29 Founding Nations

Twenty-nine countries — including Brazil, Russia, Indonesia, Pakistan, Kazakhstan, Belarus, Serbia, Cuba, and Venezuela — signed an agreement in Shanghai on July 16 establishing the World AI Cooperation Organization (WAICO), a China-headquartered body positioned as an alternative to Western-led AI governance frameworks. Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi and founding-member representatives signed the accord in the presence of UN Secretary-General António Guterres. China committed to provide 5,000 AI training slots for developing countries over five years and to establish cooperation centers with ASEAN, the African Union, CELAC, the SCO, and BRICS.

Why it mattersThe world now has two competing institutional frameworks for AI governance — one anchored in Brussels/Washington/G7, the other assembling around Shanghai/BRICS — which will matter for any multinational team navigating divergent compliance regimes.

Try itIf you operate in any WAICO founding-member market, watch for that country's domestic AI rules to start referencing WAICO standards rather than (or alongside) the EU AI Act or US frameworks.

IBM

IBM Shares Crash 25% — Worst Day on Record — as Clients Shift Spend to AI Infrastructure

IBM shares plummeted 25% on July 14 after preliminary Q2 results fell short of expectations, the company's worst trading day on record (surpassing its prior worst day, October 19, 1987). CEO Arvind Krishna told investors that in late June, clients shifted quarterly capex toward servers, storage, and memory purchases to secure supply-constrained AI infrastructure ahead of expected price increases, causing large software and consulting deals to slip. IBM guided to Q2 revenue of $17.2B and adjusted EPS of $2.93, both below consensus.

Why it mattersA historic single-day crash driven explicitly by enterprise budgets rotating from software toward AI compute/memory hardware is a hard data point that the AI buildout is now large enough to visibly reallocate capex away from adjacent tech categories.

Try itIf you sell enterprise software, model how much of your pipeline risk is 'AI infrastructure priority' rather than budget cuts — the two require very different competitive responses.

Apple

Apple Sues OpenAI and Two Former Employees Over Alleged Hardware Trade-Secret Theft

Apple filed suit in Northern California federal court on July 10-11 against OpenAI and two former Apple employees, alleging a coordinated scheme to steal hardware trade secrets tied to OpenAI's io Products consumer-hardware venture (co-founded by former Apple design chief Jony Ive). The complaint alleges OpenAI's hardware chief Tang Tan — a former Apple VP — coached departing Apple staff on evading security processes, and that job candidates were told to bring 'actual parts' from Apple to interviews; Apple says over 400 former employees now work at OpenAI. OpenAI says it has 'no interest in other companies' trade secrets.'

Why it mattersA trade-secret suit alleging systematic, coached IP extraction — not just ordinary poaching — raises the stakes for any AI lab hiring heavily from a single competitor's hardware or design teams.

Try itIf you're building a hardware-adjacent AI product with recent hires from a competitor, get clear on documented IP provenance now — discovery in a suit like this can reach further back than most teams expect.

Sysdig

Sysdig Documents 'JadePuffer' — the First Fully Autonomous AI Ransomware Attack

Cloud security firm Sysdig documented an extortion operation, dubbed JadePuffer, in which an AI agent handled the entire technical execution of a ransomware attack — exploiting a Langflow remote-code-execution flaw (CVE-2025-3248), stealing credentials, moving laterally through the network, encrypting files, and writing its own ransom note, adapting its tactics in real time after failed attempts. A human still chose the victim, provisioned infrastructure, and supplied credentials, but the technical attack chain ran without operator input.

Why it mattersThis is the first documented case where an AI agent — not a human operator running a script — performed the full technical chain of a real-world cyberattack, reasoning through obstacles like a human hacker would.

Try itSecurity teams should check exposure to CVE-2025-3248 (Langflow RCE) specifically, and treat 'agentic' attack chains as a new threat category worth its own detection rules, not just a faster version of existing ransomware playbooks.

Future of Life Institute

Future of Life Institute's Summer 2026 AI Safety Index: Top Grade Is a C+

The Future of Life Institute published its Summer 2026 AI Safety Index on July 7, grading nine leading AI companies across 37 indicators in six domains on evidence collected through June 3. Anthropic took the top overall grade at C+ (2.66), leading five of six domains; OpenAI followed at C (2.28) and Google DeepMind at C (2.01); Meta landed at D+, with xAI, DeepSeek, and Mistral receiving failing grades. Reviewers noted that several labs, including Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, and Meta, have weakened or eliminated earlier commitments to pause development if systems approached specified danger thresholds.

Why it mattersAn independent, standardized report card showing that no frontier lab clears a C+ — and that several have quietly walked back pause commitments — is a concrete data point for any team weighing vendor risk beyond raw capability benchmarks.

Try itIf you're doing AI vendor due diligence, pull the full domain-by-domain breakdown (not just the headline letter grade) for whichever labs you depend on — transparency and governance scores vary more than the overall grade suggests.
