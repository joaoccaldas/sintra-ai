OpenAI Previews GPT-5.6 Family: Sol, Terra, and Luna

OpenAI began a limited preview of the GPT-5.6 series — Sol (flagship), Terra (balanced everyday model), and Luna (fast and affordable) — advancing the frontier on software engineering, computer use, professional knowledge work, scientific research, and cybersecurity. API pricing per 1M tokens is $5/$30 (input/output) for Sol, $2.50/$15 for Terra, and $1/$6 for Luna; during the preview the models are available via the OpenAI API and Codex to a limited group of trusted partners, with general availability planned in the coming weeks. The GPT-5.6 Preview System Card was published June 25.

Why it mattersA three-tier family — flagship, balanced, and budget — lets teams match model cost to task instead of defaulting to the most expensive option, with Terra explicitly pitched at GPT-5.5-class quality for everyday work at lower cost.

Try itWhen the preview opens up, route your highest-volume, low-complexity calls to Luna or Terra and reserve Sol for genuinely hard reasoning/agentic steps — then compare blended cost against your current single-model setup.

Anthropic's 'Cadences' Economic Index Report Links Claude Usage to Daily Rhythms

Anthropic published its Economic Index report 'Cadences,' showing how the rhythms of the external world shape Claude usage — work queries subside on weekends, news requests peak in the morning, sleep-advice requests peak around 5 a.m., and tax requests surge near filing deadlines. The report draws on chat and Cowork conversations sampled April 10–June 10, 2026, and links about 9,700 survey respondents to their usage data using privacy-preserving methods, finding most respondents expect significant AI progress over the next year.

Try itSkim the report's time-of-day and weekday patterns to sanity-check assumptions about when your own users or team actually reach for AI — it may not match your roadmap's assumptions.

OpenAI and Broadcom Unveil 'Jalapeño,' OpenAI's First Custom Inference Chip

OpenAI and Broadcom unveiled Jalapeño, OpenAI's first custom AI accelerator, a blank-slate design built specifically for LLM inference rather than adapted from general-purpose AI hardware. Co-developed from initial design to tape-out in roughly nine months, OpenAI says early results show significantly better performance-per-watt than current state-of-the-art alternatives, with initial deployment targeted by the end of 2026. Broadcom handled silicon and networking; Celestica contributed board, rack, and system integration.

Why it mattersA purpose-built inference ASIC is OpenAI's clearest move yet to control its own compute stack and cut dependence on Nvidia, and a nine-month design-to-tape-out cycle signals how fast custom silicon can now ship when the buyer co-designs it.

Try itTrack whether Jalapeño's end-of-2026 deployment translates into lower OpenAI API inference pricing — custom inference silicon is ultimately a bet on cost-per-token.

The European Commission selected the EUROPA consortium, led by Italian company Domyn, as winner of its Frontier AI Grand Challenge launched in February 2026. Gallup

Gallup: Tech Workers Who Skip AI Face Triple the Layoff Risk

A Gallup study based on its Feb. 4–19, 2026 survey of 23,717 US employees (including 660 who reported being unemployed after their jobs were eliminated) found tech workers who use AI at least monthly face a predicted layoff risk of about 6%, versus 18% for those who use it less often — three times higher. Separately, 31% of tech-sector workers believe their job is somewhat or very likely to be eliminated within five years due to technology, AI, or automation, versus 18% of US workers overall.

Why it mattersThis is one of the first large-sample studies to quantify a direct link between individual AI usage frequency and real-world layoff risk, giving workers and managers a concrete data point in the AI-and-jobs debate rather than speculation.

Try itIf your team hasn't mandated regular AI tool usage, treat this data as a prompt to build monthly-or-more AI usage into actual workflows, not just optional training.

OpenAI Releases LifeSciBench, a 750-Task Life-Science Research Benchmark

OpenAI published LifeSciBench on June 17, 2026, a 750-task benchmark built with 173 PhD-level scientists, grading models on free-response life-science research tasks — including genomic sequence files, chemical structures, and experimental figures — against expert-written rubrics averaging 25 criteria each (19,020 criteria total). The strongest model passed only 36.1% of all tasks and just 28.1% of artifact-heavy tasks, identifying scientific-artifact processing as the primary bottleneck for current AI systems.

Why it mattersA sub-40% pass rate from the strongest model on real-world, artifact-heavy science tasks is a useful corrective against benchmark scores on cleaner multiple-choice biology tests.

Try itIf evaluating models for scientific-research copilot use cases, test them on artifact-heavy tasks (raw sequence files, figures) rather than relying on multiple-choice benchmark scores alone.
